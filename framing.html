Framing Techniques!



Rule of Thirds

One of the most basic, but easily utilizable framing techniques, is the “Rule of Thirds”. The rule of thirds is a simple way to attract focus to a point in your photo in a way that feels natural. This rule breaks the composition down into three equal portions both vertically and horizontally. The four points where a horizontal and vertical line intersect are the points to which which your main subject should line up. The rule of thirds is informed by the mathematical concept of the “golden ratio,” which appeals to the human eye. The rule of thirds breaks your composition into thirds, effectively applying the gold ratio to your composition. An example of this is a horizon line that lines up with one of the two horizontal lines. Another simple way to improve your photographs is to “fill the frame” with your subject. Make sure that the focus of your photo takes up the majority of the space of the photo. While this sounds obvious, it's something that many amateur photographers struggle with, and a simple rule that can quickly separate an amateur from a professional photographer.

Think Opposite

One of my favorite photographers, Peter Mckinnon, encouraged his followers to “think opposite.” This advice may sound simple and self explanatory but is incredibly important to keep in mind as a photographer. Peter states that while there may be an obvious way to photograph something, if you “think opposite,” you can find a new, inventive point of view to a situation. Whether you use a different lens, different angle, or something else entirely is up to the you and the effect you are trying to have. In short, it means take time to frame a picture and give it some aspect that makes it unique.
